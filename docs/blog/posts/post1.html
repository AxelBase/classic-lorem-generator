The History of Lorem Ipsum: From Cicero to Modern Design

Published: November 20, 2025

Every designer has seen it: “Lorem ipsum dolor sit amet…” But few know it’s not random gibberish — it’s real Latin from 45 BC.

The text comes from Marcus Tullius Cicero’s philosophical treatise De Finibus Bonorum et Malorum (“On the Ends of Good and Evil”), written in 45 BC. In the 1500s, an unknown printer scrambled sections of this work to create a type specimen book. The result? A scrambled but natural-looking Latin text that has been used for over 500 years.

Why the Classic Version Still Wins

Modern “lorem ipsum” generators often create fake words or repetitive patterns. But the original text has something they don’t: authentic word length distribution, sentence rhythm, and punctuation flow — exactly like real prose.

This is why professional designers and typographers prefer the classic version. It doesn’t distract with readable English, yet it feels natural. Letters like “h”, “t”, and “e” appear in realistic proportions. Ascenders and descenders balance perfectly. Your layout looks real from the very first mockup.

Fun Fact

The phrase “Lorem Ipsum” itself doesn’t appear in Cicero’s work — it’s a corruption of “dolorem ipsum” (“pain itself”) caused by the printer cutting off the beginning and end of lines.

Next time you use placeholder text, remember: you’re not just filling space — you’re using a 2,000-year-old piece of design history.
